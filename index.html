1. ABSTRACT

This project evaluates how packet scheduling can enforce Quality of Service (QoS) for bidirectional UDP traffic in a centralized control system. Using ns-3, a single orchestrator exchanges control traffic with multiple followers across a shared bottleneck while reverse-direction status, telemetry, bulk, and competing background traffic contend for the same constrained resource. Five queueing disciplines were compared: FIFO, PFIFO_FAST-style priority FIFO, strict-priority PRIO, FQ-PIE, and FQ-CoDel. The study further examined the effect of enabling DSCP marking and classifier-aware priority mapping.

Under overload, FIFO produced severe control degradation, reaching 40.8 ms mean control delay and 61.4% control loss at the baseline comparison point, while FQ-PIE and FQ-CoDel held control delay to 8.9 ms with zero control loss. DSCP produced the largest benefit when used with priority schedulers: PFIFO_FAST-style and PRIO improved from 40.8 ms and 61.4% control loss to 8.9 ms and 0.0% loss, respectively. Load-sweep and follower-sweep experiments showed that fair-queue schedulers remained stable as offered background load and follower count increased, whereas FIFO and non-DSCP priority configurations degraded rapidly. The results show that queue selection is not a cosmetic implementation detail in UDP-based control systems; it directly determines whether latency-sensitive control traffic survives overload conditions.

2. INTRODUCTION

Many practical networked control systems favor UDP because it avoids connection setup, retransmission delay, and protocol-level congestion behavior that may interfere with tight update cycles. In a centralized orchestrator-and-device architecture, however, the same network path often carries multiple traffic classes with very different requirements. Small control packets require low delay and low loss, while reverse-direction telemetry and status updates are valuable but can tolerate somewhat lower priority. Bulk transfers and unrelated background traffic can safely absorb the remaining capacity.

This problem is especially relevant in embedded and industrial control settings, where a supervisory node may exchange frequent small control messages with multiple field devices while also collecting status and telemetry over the same shared path. In such systems, the concern is not simply raw throughput; it is whether the network can preserve bounded delay and acceptable loss for the classes that actually influence control behavior. That makes the queue at the bottleneck a first-order design concern rather than a background implementation detail.

This combination creates a scheduling problem rather than a simple transport-choice problem. UDP itself does not classify traffic, reserve bandwidth, or prevent queue buildup. If all packets are treated identically at a shared bottleneck, short control messages can become trapped behind larger or more numerous packets, which increases queueing delay and can eventually drive packet loss. The central question of this project is therefore whether packet scheduling can enforce practical QoS separation for bidirectional UDP control and telemetry traffic without changing the application transport.

3. SYSTEM DESIGN AND METHODOLOGY

3.1 Network topology and traffic model

The simulated system contains one orchestrator, two routers connected by a bidirectional bottleneck, a set of follower nodes, and two bidirectional background-traffic endpoints. The orchestrator connects to Router A through an access link, the followers connect to Router B through individual access links, and the queueing discipline under test is installed on both directions of the Router A–Router B bottleneck. Access links run at 100 Mbps with 1 ms propagation delay, while the bottleneck is configured at 10 Mbps with 5 ms propagation delay. In the baseline experiments, the queue size is fixed at 40 packets and the simulation time is 20 s.

Four application classes are generated within the control system itself. Control traffic is sent from the orchestrator to every follower. Status, telemetry, and bulk traffic are sent from each follower back to the orchestrator. Additional bidirectional background traffic is injected across the bottleneck in order to create overload conditions.

3.2 Traffic classes and DSCP mapping

Each application class is defined by a packet size, sending rate, port range, and DSCP value. Control traffic is marked AF42 when DSCP is enabled, status is marked AF43, telemetry is marked AF41, and bulk uses CS1. Background traffic is left at default treatment.

3.3 Queueing disciplines under test

Five queueing disciplines were compared. FIFO serves as the no-priority baseline. The PFIFO_FAST-style baseline was implemented as a three-band PRIO queue with FIFO children and a classifier that emulates a PFIFO_FAST-like DSCP mapping. The PRIO case extends this idea into a four-band strict-priority hierarchy so that control, status, telemetry, and lower-priority/default traffic can be separated more explicitly. FQ-PIE and FQ-CoDel represent fair-queue / active-queue-management approaches that attempt to isolate flows and suppress persistent queue growth.

3.4 Experimental scenarios

Three scenario families were executed. The scheduler-comparison scenario fixed the bottleneck at 10 Mbps, the number of followers at 4, the queue size at 40 packets, and the background rate at 12 Mbps per direction, and then compared all five schedulers with DSCP both disabled and enabled. The load-sweep scenario fixed the follower count at 4 and varied the background rate per direction across 8, 10, 12, 14, and 16 Mbps. The follower-sweep scenario fixed the offered background load at 12 Mbps per direction and varied the number of followers across 4, 8, and 16.

3.5 Metrics and data processing

The simulator exports per-flow statistics to CSV, including transmitted and received packets, packet loss, throughput, mean delay, and mean jitter. These raw outputs are then aggregated into class-summary CSV files by grouping over scenario, scheduler, DSCP mode, follower count, background rate, and flow class. In addition to delay, loss, jitter, and throughput, the post-processing stage computes Jain fairness indices.

4. RESULTS

4.1 Baseline overloaded scheduler comparison

The baseline scheduler-comparison point uses 4 followers and 12 Mbps of background traffic per direction over a 10 Mbps bottleneck. This is the clearest view of scheduler behavior under overload. FIFO produces 40.8 ms mean control delay, 61.4% control packet loss, 1.0 ms mean jitter, and only 0.227 Mbps control throughput. FQ-PIE and FQ-CoDel reduce control delay to 8.9 ms, eliminate control loss, hold jitter at 0.2 ms, and preserve 0.589 Mbps control throughput even without DSCP.

4.2 Per-class behavior and trade-offs

Control traffic is not the only class of interest. The per-class plots reveal how each scheduler redistributes service across the system. With DSCP enabled, the fair-queue schedulers keep control, status, telemetry, and bulk delays low while still allowing background throughput to remain high.

4.3 Load sweep

The load sweep tests whether the scheduler ranking observed at the baseline point remains stable as background traffic is increased. The answer is yes. Once the offered background load reaches 10 Mbps and then exceeds the 10 Mbps bottleneck, FIFO begins to degrade rapidly, while FQ-PIE and FQ-CoDel remain essentially flat across the full sweep.

4.4 Follower sweep

The follower sweep studies scaling pressure from the application side rather than the background-load side. As the number of followers increases from 4 to 8 and then 16, the same broad ranking remains visible.

4.5 Fairness analysis

Fairness was evaluated using Jain indices computed per application class. The fairness plots complement the scheduler comparison by showing that strong control protection can coexist with very different service-distribution patterns.

5. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS

This project demonstrates that QoS enforcement for bidirectional UDP control traffic is fundamentally a queue-management problem. When all packets share the bottleneck in a best-effort manner, control performance can become unacceptable even in a simple topology.

The strongest practical conclusion is that two different solution families work well in this setting. FQ-PIE and FQ-CoDel provide robust control protection even without DSCP-specific tuning in the presented topology. PFIFO_FAST-style and PRIO can also achieve very strong control behavior, but only when DSCP marking is enabled and the classifier has enough bands to separate the classes that matter.

In summary, the report finds that packet scheduling can successfully enforce QoS for bidirectional UDP-based control and telemetry traffic, but the outcome depends strongly on whether the scheduler can actually distinguish classes and prevent latency-sensitive traffic from being trapped in a common backlog. For the topology and workloads studied here, FQ-PIE, FQ-CoDel, and DSCP-enabled PRIO were the most effective configurations.
